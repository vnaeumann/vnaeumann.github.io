# July Readings

## Week 1: 1–5 July

### [Strong leaders or strong institutions?](https://substack.com/home/post/p-203952416)
**Rating:** 10/10

Talks about the importance of having strong and independent institutions and how strongman leaders borrow (or rather steal) power from institutions and more often than not leave them hollow shells of their former selves.

Good leaders create good institutions. Good institutions create good times. Good times create complacency. Complacency leads to bad times. Bad times give rise to strongmen. Strongmen weaken institutions.

### [Did liberalization doom the Congress?](https://substack.com/home/post/p-195529727)

A very interesting article. It argues that the 1991 liberalization of the Indian economy shifted votes away from the Congress—not primarily to the BJP, but to regional, caste-based parties such as BSP and SP—contributing to the rise of caste-based politics alongside the Mandal Commission.

Congress lost support where liberalization hurt the most. Liberalization benefited people with the education needed to move from agriculture into factory or salaried jobs, while hurting those without such opportunities, forcing many back into agriculture from blue-collar work.

### [A Deep Dive into the Textile Industry](https://thedailybrief.zerodha.com/p/a-deep-dive-into-the-textile-industry)

Explains why, despite being the world's largest cotton producer, India has struggled to capture the garment industry.

From growing cotton to manufacturing garments, value is added at each stage, but the largest share is created during garment manufacturing through labour and skills. Cotton is also highly volatile compared to man-made fibres, making the business less predictable.

Labour laws, factory regulations, MSME policies, and restrictions on biotechnology companies charging for seeds have limited efficient scaling and innovation. The outlook may improve as Bangladesh faces tariffs and India negotiates an FTA with the EU.

### [Can We Find Jobs for the Next Billion Workers?](https://thedailybrief.zerodha.com/p/a-deep-dive-into-the-textile-industry)

Developing countries have grown rapidly, but employment growth has remained weak.

In India, much of this is due to informality. The informal sector (including self-employment) acts as a buffer when wage jobs are unavailable. It absorbs economic shocks in both directions, keeping employment figures relatively stable even when livelihoods remain fragile.

Economic growth often shifts workers from informal to formal employment rather than creating entirely new jobs. Countries experiencing investment booms tend to see stronger employment growth.

### [How Much Irony Is Too Much Irony?](https://substack.com/home/post/p-187766583)

**Must read.**

Argues that irony once served as a shield for deeper truths but has increasingly become an end in itself. People now make spectacles of themselves at the expense of their own dignity for the amusement of others, allowing them to avoid confronting responsibility and accountability.

It also discusses how irony can humanize genuinely harmful people while infantilizing their malice.

> "Irony stopped being a weapon against the system and became the system's favourite costume."

> "If you always speak with a smirk, you can never be pinned down."
